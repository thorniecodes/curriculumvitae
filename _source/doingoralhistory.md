## Recording Technologies {#recordings}

### What is covered in the workshop?

- Understand what typical equipment is used for either in-person and Zoom online interviews
- Develop a sense of what you probably need for your oral history project
- Specific considerations needed when recording interviews over Zoom or in-person
- Considerations for cloud storage to manage your recordings and project components
- Considerations for editing video and audio interview recordings
- Experience what it is like to move a Zoom recording from Zoom's Cloud Recording storage space to your project cloud storage space

### Seriously... You Need 2 Recording Devices

No Matter What You Need 2 Recording Devices. **Why?** Because one could stop working during the interview.

### Recording Your Interview: Zoom vs. In-Person

| Consideration | Zoom — Questions to ask yourself | In-Person — Questions to ask yourself |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical Location** | Is Zoom the only way I can reach this person? How do I optimize both my physical location and the narrator's? | Can I physically access them? How optimal is their physical location? |
| **Internet Access** | Do we both have internet connections that can support a smooth Zoom video recording? As an alternative, do we both have the ability to conduct an audio Zoom interview over the phone? | You don't need the internet. |
| **Narrator Preferences** | Are there personal preferences or physical limitations that would make Zoom challenging to use? Would they prefer video or audio interviewing? Would they like someone to be there with them? Do they have photographs or objects to include? | Would they prefer video or audio interviewing? Would they like someone to be there with them? Do they have photographs or objects to include? |
| **Funding Travel & Recording Equipment** | Do you have a Zoom subscription? (You will need one.) Do you have an additional recording device, like a smartphone? | Does your project cover the cost of travel? What equipment do you need to record in person? |
| **File Sizes (Video vs. Audio)** | Zoom files tend to be relatively small for both video and audio. | In-person video files can be large to quite large. Audio files can also be large, but are almost always smaller than video. |
| **Tech Support** | Do you know how to create and record a meeting? Do you know how to find your file after the recording? Do you know how to work with your files once transferred? | Do you know how to operate your equipment? Do you know how to transfer your files? Do you know how to work with your files once transferred? |

### Common File Types for Recordings

**Audio File Formats:**
- **MP3** — widely-used compressed audio format; good balance between file size and quality
- **M4A** — uncompressed audio made by Apple for high-quality recording and editing; can open on a PC
- **WAV** — uncompressed audio format often used for high-quality recording and editing

**Video File Formats:**
- **MP4** — widely-used container format holding compressed video (H.264) and audio (AAC)
- **MOV** — multimedia container developed by Apple; compatible with macOS and Windows
- **AVI** — multimedia container introduced by Microsoft; limitations in compression and file size

### Roles & Responsibilities {#roles}

> "These roles may overlap or be combined depending on the size and scope of the oral history project."

1. **Project Manager** — oversees entire project, coordinates team, sets goals and timelines, manages budget and resources
2. **Interviewer** — conducts interviews, develops questions, ensures participant comfort
3. **Transcriptionist** — converts recordings to written form, ensures accuracy, may edit and proofread
4. **Archivist or Librarian** — organizes materials, catalogs items, develops access systems, ensures preservation
5. **Researcher or Historian** — conducts background research, assists with questions and participant selection, analyzes significance
6. **Technology Specialist** — oversees technical recording/storage aspects, ensures audio/video quality, provides technical support
7. **Outreach and Engagement Coordinator** — promotes project, develops engagement strategies, organizes events and publications

### Permission Forms {#permissions}

"Permission forms are essential for ensuring the legal and ethical integrity of an oral history project, protecting the rights of the participants, and facilitating the long-term preservation and use of the collected interviews."

**Six core reasons for permission forms:**

1. **Legal and Ethical Compliance** — ensures compliance with guidelines regarding personal information and intellectual property; demonstrates participants received project information and understand their rights; provides evidence of voluntary consent

2. **Clarity of Terms** — outlines terms, conditions, use, storage, and access of interviews; specifies restrictions such as embargoes, anonymity requirements, and sensitive content; prevents misunderstandings between teams and participants

3. **Protection of Participants' Rights** — informs about withdrawal rights, anonymity options, and use restrictions; details personal data handling and protection; may include content review and approval provisions

4. **Future Use and Access** — grants necessary rights for preservation, use, and sharing; enables inclusion in archives, publications, exhibitions, and online platforms; facilitates long-term accessibility for researchers and the public

5. **Institutional Requirements** — universities, libraries, archives, and funding agencies often require signed forms; demonstrates adherence to institutional policies and best practices

6. **Building Trust** — creates dialogue about project goals between teams and participants; provides opportunity to address concerns; enhances project credibility and integrity

### File Naming Standardization {#filenames}

"When establishing a file naming standard for an oral history project, it's essential to create a consistent, clear, and informative naming convention that allows for easy identification and organization of the files."

**Elements of a file naming standard:**

1. **Project Identifier** — short code or acronym representing the project. Example: `OHP_`
2. **Interviewee Identifier** — unique identifier such as initials. Example: `OHP_JDS_`
3. **Interview Date** — format YYYYMMDD for chronological sorting. Example: `OHP_JDS_20210315_`
4. **Interview Type** — audio, video, transcript, or additional materials. Example: `OHP_JDS_20210315_Audio`
5. **Part Number** — if the interview spans multiple files. Example: `OHP_JDS_20210315_Audio_Part1`
6. **File Extension** — appropriate extension for the file type. Example: `OHP_JDS_20210315_Audio_Part1.mp3`
7. **Avoid Special Characters** — use underscores (`_`) instead of spaces; refrain from punctuation marks or special characters
8. **Keep It Short** — long file names can cause issues with some systems

**Complete example:** `OHP_JDS_20210315_Audio_Part1.mp3`
This file name indicates: oral history project, interviewee John D. Smith, recorded March 15, 2021, audio file, first part of the interview.

"Remember to document your file naming standard and ensure that all team members adhere to it consistently throughout the project."

### Folder Naming & Structure {#folders}

"When creating a folder structure for an oral history project, it's important to establish a clear, organized, and intuitive hierarchy that allows for easy navigation and file management."

**Folder structure instructions:**

1. **Main Project Folder** — descriptive name as parent directory. Example: `Oral_History_Project`
2. **Interviewee Folders** — one per interviewee using consistent naming. Example: `Oral_History_Project/Smith_John_D`
3. **Interview Date Folders** — subfolders in YYYYMMDD format. Example: `Oral_History_Project/Smith_John_D/20210315`
4. **File Type Folders** — subfolders for Audio, Video, Transcripts, Additional_Materials. Example: `Oral_History_Project/Smith_John_D/20210315/Audio`
5. **Consistent Naming** — use underscores; avoid spaces, punctuation, and special characters throughout
6. **Access and Permissions** — create folders for managing team access and shared resources. Example: `Oral_History_Project/Team_Resources`
7. **Backup and Archive** — separate folder structure for backup copies. Example: `Oral_History_Project/Backups`
8. **Documentation** — folder for guidelines, protocols, release forms, metadata templates. Example: `Oral_History_Project/Documentation`

"Remember to document your folder naming best practices and ensure that all team members follow the established structure consistently throughout the project."

### Project Log {#projectlog}

**Why is a Project Log important?**

1. **Documentation and Context** — provides critical documentation about the oral history project
2. **Search and Discovery** — rich metadata descriptions make the oral histories more discoverable
3. **Preservation and Use** — comprehensive metadata is essential for long-term digital preservation
4. **Context of Creation** — records specifics about how interviews were conducted
5. **Intellectual Property** — logs details like release forms, permissions, and copyright holders

"It is highly recommended that you use a spreadsheet to capture all project log information."

**Project Log Glossary:**

| Term | Definition | Why is it important? |
|---|---|---|
| Interview Session Title | Contains interview name, date, and possibly theme | Provides significant details; disambiguates multiple interviews |
| Narrator Name | First and last name of narrator | Ensures correct spelling; helps differentiate individuals |
| Name of Interviewer | Person who conducted interview | Retains context of who asked questions |
| Location of Interview | Place where interview occurred | Setting impacts interview and research |
| Date of Interview | Month, Day, Year (optionally time) | Situates interview historically |
| Length of Interview | Duration in hours, minutes, seconds | Important for management and reader purposes |
| Interview Summary | Brief overview of topics discussed | Helps readers judge engagement value |
| Short Biography | Life arc sketch of narrator | Helps readers understand context |
| File Formats | Types of digitally captured materials | Informs technical characteristics |
| File Storage Location | Physical/virtual storage spaces with links | Crucial for safeguarding files |
| Rights | Ownership, access, reuse specifications | Ensures ethical respect of narrator wishes |
| Keywords | Main themes, locations, events | Provides quick access and archival benefit |
| Special Considerations | Post-interview notes and edits | Crucial for post-interview processing |
| File Name | Unique identifier for interview components | Standardized naming aids organization |
| Language | Language(s) used in interview | May indicate translation needs |
| Transcriptionist/Translator | Person responsible for transcription/translation | Useful for editing and project management |

### Photographs of Narrators (Optional) {#photographs}

**Why are photographs of the narrator important?**

1. **Visual context** — puts a face to the voice and story; creates stronger connection between narrator and audience
2. **Historical documentation** — captures narrator's appearance, clothing, and visible personal characteristics at the time of interview
3. **Humanizing the narrative** — reminds the audience that experiences and emotions shared are those of a real person; fosters empathy
4. **Enhancing exhibits and presentations** — can be used in exhibitions, presentations, and educational materials
5. **Honoring the narrator** — acknowledges their contribution and the value of their experiences
6. **Promoting diversity and representation** — showcases diversity of narrators; particularly important for marginalized or underrepresented communities
7. **Facilitating recognition and connection** — may help narrators reconnect with friends, family, or community members

"While not all narrators may feel comfortable being photographed, offering the option and explaining the importance can enhance the overall impact and value of an oral history project."

**Image File Guidance:**

- **JPEG (.jpg)** — widely used compressed format; balances quality and file size; average 500 KB to 2 MB depending on resolution
- **TIFF (.tif)** — lossless format that preserves high image quality; average 5 MB to 20 MB or more depending on resolution and color depth

"When managing these files, it's essential to consider the trade-offs between quality, file size, and long-term preservation requirements."

### Interview Notes {#interviewnotes}

**Interview notes are crucial for oral history projects for several reasons:**

1. **Accurate documentation** — help ensure information provided by the narrator is accurately documented; serve as a written record of main points and key themes
2. **Contextual information** — can include setting, narrator's body language, emotions, or other relevant observations not apparent in the recording
3. **Backup and reference** — serve as a backup in case of technical issues; can be used as quick reference when reviewing the interview or creating transcripts
4. **Identifying themes and connections** — as the interviewer takes notes, they can identify emerging themes, connections between stories, or areas requiring follow-up
5. **Facilitating analysis and interpretation** — help researchers identify patterns, compare experiences across narrators, and draw conclusions
6. **Enhancing accessibility** — provide a written summary for those without access to or ability to listen to recordings
7. **Aiding in the preservation process** — part of overall documentation; can be archived alongside recordings, transcripts, and related materials

"It is important for the interviewer to be clear, concise, and objective when documenting the information provided by the narrator. The notes should be reviewed and edited for clarity and accuracy as soon as possible after the interview."

### Transcription Files {#transcriptionfiles}

**Why are transcription files important?**

1. **Accessibility and searchability** — provide a written record that allows researchers, students, and the public to quickly scan and search for specific information without listening to the entire recording
2. **Preservation and long-term access** — while recordings can deteriorate or become obsolete, transcription files provide a stable, long-term means of preserving content
3. **Analysis and research** — facilitate analysis by allowing researchers to review, compare, and annotate content across multiple interviews; can be coded, tagged, or indexed
4. **Publication and dissemination** — often used as the basis for publications, exhibits, or educational materials; provide easy way to quote or reference specific passages
5. **Translation and language access** — can be translated into different languages for multilingual audiences
6. **Legal and ethical considerations** — may be necessary for legal or ethical reasons; accurate transcriptions help ensure adherence to relevant laws and guidelines

**Document File Guidance:**

- **Plain Text (.txt)** — simplest and most lightweight; no formatting; average a few KB to a few hundred KB
- **Rich Text Format (.rtf)** — supports basic formatting (bold, italic, underline); average a few hundred KB to a few MB
- **Microsoft Word (.doc, .docx)** — rich formatting options; widely compatible; average a few hundred KB to several MB
- **PDF (.pdf)** — universal format that preserves layout; average a few hundred KB to several MB

**Transcription Tools:**

## Transcriptions {#transcriptions}

**What are Transcriptions?**

Transcriptions consist of "The full text of your interview, clearly specifying who is talking and when."

**Example transcription:** [Aki Hill Oral History Interview, April 14, 2015](https://scarc.library.oregonstate.edu/oh150/hill/hill.pdf)

**Headers should include:**
- Narrator's name
- Interviewer's name
- Interview date
- Interview location

**Best Practices for Transcribing:**
- Omit verbal tics like "um's" and false starts
- Mark unclear words with `[?]`
- Use `[unintelligible]` for inaudible passages
- Include timestamps approximately every five minutes
- Aim to preserve the narrator's dialect while maintaining consistent formatting

**Why Transcriptions Matter:**
1. Enhanced accessibility and searchability for wider audiences
2. Long-term preservation as stable records
3. Facilitation of analysis and comparative research
4. Support for publications, exhibits, and educational uses
5. Enable translation for multilingual audiences
6. Support legal and ethical compliance

**Transcription Software Options:**

| Software | AI Used | Price | Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Trint** | Yes | $60/month | AI-assisted; good for non-English |
| **Express Scribe** | No | $60/year | Manual transcription; variable playback speeds |
| **Zoom** | Yes | $15.99/month | AI-assisted for Zoom recordings only |

## Archiving {#archiving}

### LOCKSS — Lots Of Copies Keep Stuff Safe

**L**OTS **O**F **C**OPIES **K**EEP **S**TUFF **S**AFE

"This is a basic rule of thumb for digital preservation and can be applied in many different ways, including the creation of analog derivatives (i.e., paper transcripts) from digital originals (i.e., digital audio or video recordings)."

**Why is LOCKSS important?**
- Provides a guiding principle for addressing digital preservation challenges in archives for the 21st century
- Digital materials are inherently more fragile than physical materials
- Advocates for depositing many copies in many different locations

**Don't worry about perfection:**

> "Any action is better than no action. Any plan is better than no plan when it comes to LOCKSS."

### Suggested LOCKSS Actions for Cultural Organizations

**Step 1:** Place digital files in cloud locations (Google Drive, Dropbox, Box) with documented access for multiple staff members

**Step 2:** Store multiple copies on physical media (external hard drives) in different locations, documented in project log

**Step 3:** Keep a computer copy as secondary backup; partner with outside organizations to host additional file copies

### Physical Transcripts Are Part of LOCKSS Too

- Consider donating bound paper copies to local libraries for preservation and access purposes
- Paper records are inherently more durable than digital files
- Libraries have established systems for describing and providing access to paper materials
- Materials can be cataloged in local library systems and community-networked catalogs

## Access {#access}

### Access to Interviews Can Take On Many Forms

**Varying Levels of Access:**
- Paper copies of print transcripts made available through a local library or historical society
- Digital and/or paper materials provided to researchers via an archival repository (with a dedicated archival finding aid and/or digital collection; restrictions on certain types of access can be administered by the archive)
- Unfettered access enabled by you via upload to an online media host

### Do It Yourself Access Options

**[Internet Archive](https://archive.org)**
- Pros: Free; aligned with a larger non-profit global initiative to preserve web content; shows up in Google Search results
- Cons: Cannot be undone; audio and video; the interface isn't the easiest to use

**[YouTube](https://www.youtube.com)**
- Pros: Free; the default platform for many content consumers; familiar to most people; make playlists; dedicated channel; shows up in Google Search results
- Cons: Video only; potential for ads; potential headaches with comments (though they can be turned off)

**[Vimeo](https://vimeo.com)**
- Pros: Free up to a point; make playlists; dedicated channel; shows up in Google Search results; not part of Google; usually non-profits and creative communities use it
- Cons: Video only; potential for ads; potential headaches with comments (though they can be turned off)

**[SoundCloud](https://soundcloud.com)**
- Pros: Free up to a point; make playlists; dedicated channel; shows up in Google Search results
- Cons: Audio only; potential headaches with comments (though they can be turned off)

### Vimeo vs. YouTube Comparison

| Feature | Vimeo | YouTube |
|---|---|---|
| Video quality | 4K, 8K, HDR support | Up to 8K |
| Ads | Ad free | Pre-roll and mid-roll ads |
| Video recording length | Unlimited | 15 minutes; 12 hours (128 GB) for verified users |
| Bandwidth | Up to 2 TB/month; unlimited for Enterprise | Unlimited |
| Live streaming | Yes | Yes |
| Analytics dashboard | Yes | Yes |
| Video library & team folders | Yes | No |
| Video storage | Up to 500 videos; unlimited for Enterprise | Unlimited |

## Additional Resources {#resources}

### [Oral History Association's Principles and Best Practices](https://www.oralhistory.org/principles-and-best-practices-revised-2018/#Introduction)
The organization brings together practitioners from various communities and fields. This suite of documents (updated 2018, addendum added 2019) provides guiding principles of oral history, recognizing diverse practices in collection, interpretation, use, and preservation.

### [Oral History in the Digital Age](http://ohda.matrix.msu.edu/)
For small and large organizations planning or maintaining oral history projects. Includes a Getting Started section, video essays with leading oral historians, practical equipment advice, and best practices guidelines.

### [Oral History Association's Resource List](https://oralhistory.org/resources/)
"A thorough list of resources assembled by The Oral History Association" to assist those interested in oral history.

### [UCI Oral History Toolkit](https://oralhistory.lib.uci.edu/)
University of California, Irvine Libraries offers a free resource with six modules, designed for conducting single interviews or implementing large-scale community documentation projects.

### [Texas Tech Oral History Guide](https://guides.library.ttu.edu/c.php?g=1275281&p=9356267)
Created by Texas Tech University Libraries, covering in-person and remote interviews, recording equipment, release forms, transcription, and additional guidance.

### [The Veterans History Project at the Library of Congress](http://www.loc.gov/vets/vets-home.html)
Offers opportunity for military families to preserve veteran memories. Provides step-by-step guidelines including the [VHP Fieldkit Companion Video](http://www.loc.gov/vets/rams/vhp-fieldkit.ram) covering interviewing, supporting materials selection, and submission procedures.

### [StoryCorps](http://storycorps.org/)
Provides Americans of all backgrounds and beliefs with the opportunity to record, share, and preserve the stories of our lives. Includes the [Military Voices Initiative](http://storycorps.org/initiatives/military-voices-initiative/) which aims to collect the stories of post-9/11 veterans, active-duty service members, and their families.
