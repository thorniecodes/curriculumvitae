## Scholarly Communications Connecting Academia and Community

### A New Approach to Scholarly Communication

Public scholarship is transforming higher education, redefining academic influence and fostering deeper engagement between institutions and communities outside its walls. This scholarly communication approach emphasizes community-based research, where colleges and universities collaborate closely with local, state, and regional partners to address real-world issues.

### Community Engaged Research: The Foundation of Public Scholarship

Community-based research is at the heart of this transformation, involving:

- Co-identifying problems with community partners like non-profits, government agencies, and affinity groups
- Collaboratively designing research projects
- Shared decision-making and stewardship in data collection and analysis
- Inclusive and accessible publishing of findings
- Developing and implementing action plans within the community

*Learn more about participatory research methodologies: Vaughn, L. M., & Jacquez, F. (2020). Participatory Research Methods – Choice Points in the Research Process. Journal of Participatory Research Methods, 1(1). https://doi.org/10.35844/001c.13244*

### The Role of Academic Libraries

Academic libraries play a crucial role in this ecosystem, serving as bridges between scholarly work and public benefit. They champion accessible scholarly communications by democratizing knowledge through liaison activities and relationships among networks where partnerships flourish.

*Learn more: [ACRL Keeping Up With… Public Scholarship: Reimagining Scholarly Communication for Societal Impact](https://www.ala.org/acrl/publications/keeping_up_with/public_scholarship)*

### Benefits to Society

This collaborative approach:

- Enhances the relevance of higher education
- Strengthens accountability to the public
- Shifts from traditional academic models to engaged scholarship
- Creates direct societal benefits through applied research

*Learn more: [National Alliance for the Humanities Humanities for All initiative](https://humanitiesforall.org/)*

## Partnerships & Collaboration

### Faculty-Librarian Partnerships in Public Scholarship

When faculty and academic librarians collaborate as equal partners, they create powerful public scholarship initiatives with greater reach and impact.

**What Faculty Bring:**

- Subject matter expertise and research methodology
- Classroom teaching and student engagement opportunities
- Grant-writing experience and funding connections
- Disciplinary networks and professional associations
- Theoretical frameworks and scholarly context

**What Librarians Bring:**

- Library and Information Science disciplinary expertise
- Information discovery and resource management expertise
- Digital tools consultations for research and teaching
- Digital publishing platforms and digital repositories
- Community engagement and outreach experience
- Digital, information, visual, and data literacy education
- Data management and visualization capabilities
- Public access advocacy and copyright knowledge
- Digital collections and website development expertise

**Partnership Benefits:** Together, these complementary skill sets allow for more robust community-based research projects. Faculty provide deep disciplinary knowledge while librarians contribute information infrastructure expertise. This equal partnership model creates public scholarship that effectively bridges academic and community needs, resulting in accessible, impactful research that addresses real-world challenges.

### Community Partners in Public Scholarship

When faculty, librarians, and community members collaborate as equal partners, public scholarship achieves its fullest potential. This three-way partnership creates meaningful research that directly addresses community needs.

**What Community Members Bring:**

- Local knowledge and lived experience
- Real-world problem identification and priority setting
- Cultural context and community networks
- Practical implementation expertise
- Accountability mechanisms and feedback loops

**How Community Members Contribute:**

- Help identify research questions relevant to actual needs
- Participate in data collection and interpretation
- Share indigenous and local knowledge often missing from academic discourse
- Test solutions in real-world settings
- Ensure research outputs are accessible and useful

**Partnership Benefits:** This inclusive model transforms traditional research into truly collaborative public scholarship. Community members provide essential perspectives that academic partners might miss, while faculty contribute methodological rigor and librarians offer information infrastructure. Together, they create knowledge that is not only academically sound but also immediately applicable to community challenges.

**Sustainable Impact:** When community members are equal partners from the beginning, research outcomes are more likely to be implemented, sustained, and championed at the local level. This approach builds lasting relationships that extend beyond single projects, creating an ongoing cycle of community-engaged scholarship.

### From Service to Collaboration: Librarians as Equal Partners in Public Scholarship

The role of academic librarians is evolving significantly, moving beyond traditional service models toward authentic collaboration and partnership with faculty and communities.

**The Traditional Service Model:** Historically, librarians operated primarily in a service capacity—responding to requests, providing resources, and supporting the work of others without equal input in the scholarly process.

**The Partnership Evolution:** Today's librarians are stepping into collaborative roles where they:

- Co-create research agendas rather than simply supporting them
- Actively participate in grant writing instead of just providing resources
- Design methodologies alongside faculty rather than following their lead
- Share authorship and recognition for scholarly outputs
- Contribute unique expertise rather than solely facilitating others' work

**Equal Partners in Knowledge Creation:** In this collaborative model, librarians bring distinctive skills that complement faculty expertise:

- Information architecture and knowledge organization
- Digital scholarship tools and preservation strategies
- Scholarly communication networks and open access advocacy
- Data management and visualization capabilities
- Cross-disciplinary perspectives that bridge academic silos
- Grant writing that ties information access and preservation into initiatives

**Community Engagement Leadership:** Librarians often excel at:

- Building sustainable community relationships over time
- Translating academic content for broader audiences
- Creating accessible platforms for knowledge sharing
- Facilitating dialogue across different stakeholder groups
- Ensuring continuity of projects beyond initial research phases

This shift from service providers to equal partners transforms not only the librarian's role but enhances the impact and sustainability of public scholarship initiatives. By recognizing librarians' unique contributions, institutions create more robust and effective collaborative teams capable of addressing complex community challenges.

### Student Engagement in Public Scholarship

Students are becoming vital contributors to public scholarship initiatives, moving beyond traditional classroom roles to become active co-creators of community-based research.

**Authentic Learning Through Real Projects:** When students engage in public scholarship:

- They develop projects with real-world applications rather than hypothetical assignments
- Their work directly addresses community-identified needs
- They see the immediate impact of their academic efforts
- They build tangible portfolio pieces showcasing practical skills

**Professional Development Benefits:**

- Hands-on practice with research methodologies and tools
- Experience collaborating with diverse stakeholders
- Project management and problem-solving in complex environments
- Communication skills across different audiences and contexts
- Technical skills in data collection, analysis, and presentation

**Career Preparation:** Students gain competitive advantages for future employment:

- Documented experience working with diverse teams and partners
- Demonstrated ability to translate academic knowledge into practical solutions
- Professional networking opportunities beyond campus
- Understanding of ethical considerations in community work
- Experience navigating organizational partnerships

**Skills Development Beyond Coursework:**

- Digital literacy and technological competencies
- Critical thinking applied to real-world problems
- Cultural competence and community engagement skills
- Professional communication across diverse audiences
- Collaborative teamwork across disciplines

### Invisible No More: Celebrating Mesoamerican Languages and Communities in Diaspora in Oregon | 2024–2027

Invisible No More! (2024–2027) features open-access digital tools such as a map, database, and oral histories to serve both Oregon's Mesoamerican communities and the human services, non-profits, and state agencies that support them. The project seeks to highlight the importance of the diverse languages and cultures of Indigenous Mesoamerican peoples in Oregon, and in doing so, contribute to cultural, linguistic, and social justice.

**Funding:** Andrew W. Mellon Foundation and UO Board of Trustees
**Initiative Type:** Research
**Website:** https://invisiblenomore.uoregon.edu

**Key Partners in the Collaboration:**

- Department of Anthropology
- Department of Linguistics
- UO Libraries
- UO Infographics
- Various community non-profits and community members

**What Will Be Accomplished:** The project will create digital stories enriched with images and audio, develop oral history collections, craft digital maps, and compile ethnobotanical image collections. All resources will be designed with input and feedback from non-profit community partners, serving both Oregon's Mesoamerican communities and service providers by improving access to linguistic and cultural materials for schools, healthcare services, and public agencies.

**How UO Libraries Is Supporting the Research:** UO Libraries provides expert support through specialized digital infrastructure and technical guidance. It delivers comprehensive assistance with digital preservation, multimedia storytelling technologies, intellectual property considerations, and sustainable data management workflows. Additionally, it supports accessible publishing platforms for cultural materials while GIS specialists support the digital mapping components. Working closely with community partners, we ensure all technological implementations honor cultural protocols and community ownership, creating meaningful digital representations of Mesoamerican heritage.

**Impact Beyond Campus** *(for official release in 2027):*

- OHSU speech pathologist connected with the team to find people who speak Northern Mam close to a patient's home.
- Oregon Historical Society's Ethnic Studies Educator working on a unit on Latine history in Oregon for middle and high school students wanting to show Mesoamerican map to understand the language diversity of Latine people in Oregon.

### Oral History Training for The Ethics of Sharing Results with Research Participants: Establishing Best Practices for Development Economics | Summer 2024

In Summer 2024, oral history training was provided to the research initiative, "The Ethics of Sharing Results with Research Participants: Establishing Best Practices for Development Economics." Working with a team of principal investigators, the training prepared graduate students and researchers from UC Berkeley and the University of Oregon to conduct effective semi-structured interviews.

*From left to right: Michell Martinez (UO Libraries), Kate Thornhill (UO Libraries), Melissa Graboyles (UO History), and Alfredo Burlando (UO Economics)*

The training equipped researchers with practical interviewing skills for both in-person and Zoom environments, focusing on ethical considerations in cross-cultural contexts and techniques for eliciting meaningful responses. This training directly supported the project's Year 2 objectives to gather perspectives from economists and African research participants through 120 interviews. The training helped prepare the research team to explore how results from Randomized Controlled Trials are shared with participants on the African continent, ultimately working toward developing best practices for research dissemination.

**Funding:** National Science Foundation ($399,000 over 3 years)
**Initiative Type:** Research

**Key Partners in the Collaboration:**

- University of Oregon Department of History
- University of Oregon Department of Economics
- UC Berkeley's Center for Effective Global Action (CEGA)
- UO Libraries

**What Will Be Accomplished:** The NSF-funded project established ethical best practices for sharing research results with participants in development economics. The research includes a systematic review of literature on dissemination practices, 400 surveys and 120 semi-structured interviews with economists and African participants, and the development of Best Practices for Research Dissemination. The project aims to change norms around how results from Randomized Controlled Trials are shared with participants on the African continent.

**How UO Libraries Is Supporting the Research:** UO Libraries provides oral history training that delivers essential methodological support for the research team. The training equips graduate students and researchers with skills to conduct effective semi-structured interviews both in-person and via Zoom. The curriculum covers ethical considerations in cross-cultural research contexts, techniques for eliciting meaningful responses, and processes for recruitment, consent, and data management. This support directly enables the Year 2 research objectives of gathering diverse perspectives through interviews.

**Impact Beyond Campus:** The project has significant broader impacts beyond the academic environment. The research will establish new norms for sharing results through publications, blog posts, conference presentations, and a special journal issue on RCT Research Ethics. The findings will be integrated into existing courses, reaching an estimated 900 students. Additionally, practical trainings will be offered to build dissemination skills at CEGA Research Transparency and Reproducibility Trainings, benefiting doctoral students and undergraduates at both UC Berkeley and UO.

### Casa y Comunidad | 2023–2024

Casa y Comunidad: Latino/a/x Housing in Oregon is an oral history-based limited documentary series (docuseries) about salient housing access issues affecting the Latino/a/x community in Oregon, one of the fastest-growing immigrant gateways for Mexican and Central American migrants in the U.S. In Summer 2022, an all-Latino/a/x team from and/or based in Oregon set out to document the everyday realities of the Latino/a/x community as they seek to satisfy one of the most basic of human needs—housing. The team covered the seven major regions of the State of Oregon, each of which presents a unique housing challenge.

**Funding:** Whiting Foundation and Andrew W. Mellon Foundation
**Initiative Type:** Research
**Website:** https://www.casaycomunidad.org

**Key Partners in the Collaboration:**

- School of Planning, Public Policy and Management
- UO Libraries
- Various non-profit community partners

**How UO Libraries Supported the Research:**

- Training graduate research assistants in digital and data methodologies
- Developing digital collection standards aligned with library and archive best practices
- Archiving digital research materials
- Digital platform and website development consultation

**Impact Beyond Campus:** Through targeted outreach to 750 individuals—including Oregon school administrators, nonprofit organizations, cultural institutions, libraries, and government leaders—the outreach specifically focused on connecting 9–12th grade social studies, history, and ethnic studies teachers. This strategic outreach effort demonstrates how university library-led public scholarship initiatives can directly support K-12 education and community engagement, bridging academic resources with practical classroom applications while preserving and sharing important cultural histories that might otherwise remain invisible.

The digital collection comprises over 550 unique multimedia items and provides essential teaching resources that support Oregon's state-mandated ethnic studies curriculum requirements (HB 2845/HB 2023). The Casa y Comunidad docuseries has gained additional recognition through selection at approximately 30 film festivals, both domestic and international, where it has received awards and nominations—further amplifying the project's reach and impact while driving additional traffic to the freely accessible archive housed at the UO Libraries.

### The Healers Project: Decolonizing Knowledge Within Afro-Indigenous Traditions | 2019–2024

A collaborative research project emerging from experiences of building community alongside Indigenous, Afro-descendant, and Afro-Indigenous peoples throughout Cuba, the Dominican Republic, Puerto Rico, and the U.S. Pacific Northwest region (PNW).

**Initiative Type:** Research
**Website:** https://healers.uoregon.edu

**Key Partners in the Collaboration:**

- Department of Indigenous, Race, and Ethnic Studies
- Department of Women, Gender, and Sexuality Studies
- UO Libraries Department of Open Research
- UO Digital Library Services
- UO Teaching and Learning Services
- Digital Library of the Caribbean

**How UO Libraries Supported the Research:**

- Led technology development for the research initiative
- Stewarded digital collections archiving
- Provided training and technology consultation for graduate research students
- Guided intellectual property rights management choices
- Created and implemented research data management practices

**Impact Beyond Campus:**

- Healers featured on the website feel heard, seen, and represented
- Website is used in K-12 classrooms across Oregon and at the UO in graduate and undergraduate classes as an open educational resource
- The website has reached over 8,000 unique users since 2019
- Digital collections are part of the Digital Library of the Caribbean

*Learn more: Gaede, F., Lara, A.-M., Reyes-Santos, A., & Thornhill, K. (2022). Afro-Indigenous Women Healers in the Caribbean and Its Diasporas: A Decolonial Digital Humanities Project. Digital Humanities Quarterly, 016(3). https://www.digitalhumanities.org/dhq/vol/16/3/000631/000631.html*

### Feeling Documents: Liberated Archives Exhibition | 2023

A collaboration between The Holding Contemporary, Don't Shoot Portland, UO Center for Art Research, and UO Libraries culminated in creating an art exhibition that transformed archival materials and documentary photography into powerful visual storytelling.

**Initiative Type:** Research

**Key Partners in the Collaboration:**

- **Holding Contemporary:** An art gallery in Portland focused on contemporary works
- **Don't Shoot Portland:** A community organization advocating for social justice
- **UO Center for Art Research:** Provided a fellowship supporting Holding Contemporary and Don't Shoot Portland's exhibition
- **UO Libraries:** Contributed specialized expertise through two key roles: the art librarian who initially connected the partners to library resources; the public scholarship librarian who provided specialized assistance with intellectual property rights

**How UO Libraries Supported the Research:** The public scholarship librarian worked directly with the community partners to:

- Help locate relevant archival images and documents
- Navigate complex access restrictions to contemporary documentary photography (e.g. journalistic photography)
- Secure appropriate licensing for exhibition use
- Ensure proper attribution and ethical use of sensitive materials

**Impact Beyond Campus:** This partnership exemplifies how university library resources and expertise can extend beyond academic walls to support community-led creative and social justice initiatives. By providing specialized knowledge in image research, rights management, and archival access, the UO Libraries helped transform scholarly resources into publicly accessible art that addresses important social issues. The exhibition created new pathways for community members to engage with historical archives in meaningful, emotionally resonant ways while building sustainable relationships between the university and community organizations.

### Environmental Justice Research Repository | 2023

This digital collection documents the history of environmental racism in the Eugene-Springfield area through a collaborative partnership between academia and community activism. Developed jointly by University of Oregon's ENG 470 Technologies & Texts Capstone students and the UO Libraries, the project archives crucial historical evidence of environmental inequities affecting marginalized communities.

**Initiative Type:** Classroom
**Website:** https://learn-static.github.io/eng-470/

Working in close consultation with Beyond Toxics, a leading environmental justice nonprofit in Oregon, this archive serves as both an educational resource and a tool for ongoing advocacy efforts. The collection brings together previously scattered research materials, creating a centralized repository that illuminates patterns of environmental discrimination while empowering communities with access to their own environmental histories. By bridging academic research, library science expertise, and grassroots environmental justice work, this digital collection exemplifies how collaborative public scholarship can address pressing social issues while preserving vital community knowledge for future generations.

**Key Partners in the Collaboration:**

- Department of English
- UO Libraries
- Beyond Toxics

**How UO Libraries Supported the Research:**

- Taught students how to use the digital archiving platform
- Provided training on critical cataloging standards and best practices
- Guided students through intellectual property rights assessment
- Instructed on applying geospatial coordinates to maps
- Facilitated the technical implementation of the digital repository
- Ensured metadata consistency across collection items with faculty partner
- Maintained quality control throughout the archiving process

**Impact Beyond Campus:**

- **Educational Resource:** Serves as a valuable teaching tool for courses on environmental justice, local history, and social activism at both university and K-12 levels.
- **Community Empowerment:** Provides Beyond Toxics with documented evidence of environmental racism, supporting advocacy efforts and policy change initiatives.
- **Skill Development:** Equipped students with practical digital archiving, cataloging, and geospatial mapping skills that transfer to professional contexts.
- **Accessibility:** Makes previously hard-to-find information about local environmental inequities publicly accessible to researchers, activists, and community members.
- **Public Awareness:** Increases visibility of environmental justice concerns in the Eugene-Springfield area, potentially influencing public opinion and policy discussions.

### Mount Hood Stories: Exploring Racial Ecologies | 2022

A collection of interactive essays showcasing research from Professor Sarah D. Wald's "Racial Ecologies" graduate seminar, created through collaboration with environmental advocacy organization Bark. The project uncovers alternative narratives about Mt. Hood National Forest by examining intersections of race, ecology, and land use, challenging conventional understandings of this significant landscape.

**Initiative Type:** Classroom
**Website:** https://blogs.uoregon.edu/mounthoodstories/

These researched stories provide fresh perspectives on Mt. Hood's complex history and present, offering new frameworks for advocates, recreational users, and land managers while creating meaningful connections between academic scholarship and community engagement. Initially featured in Bark's annual People's Forest Forum, this public scholarship initiative creates a lasting digital resource that enriches our understanding of Mt. Hood's significance across diverse communities through its multidirectional partnership between university researchers, community-based advocates, and library.

**Key Partners in the Collaboration:**

- Department of English
- UO Libraries
- BARK

**How UO Libraries Supported the Research:**

- Taught students how to use the digital storytelling platform and web accessibility practices
- Developed initiative website
- Topic research support

**Impact Beyond Campus:**

- **Enhanced Public Discourse:** Featured at Bark's annual People's Forest Forum, the project introduced alternative narratives about Mt. Hood to environmental advocates, recreational users, and land managers, enriching regional conversations about public lands and racial justice.
- **Resources for Land Management:** Provided forest managers and environmental organizations with research-based perspectives on Mt. Hood's diverse cultural histories, potentially influencing more inclusive approaches to public lands stewardship.
- **Community-Academic Bridge:** Demonstrated effective collaboration between university and an environmental advocacy organization, creating a model for community-engaged scholarship that addresses real-world environmental justice concerns.
